4 Insights into Nature-related Metrics and Target-setting along the Value Chain → How stakeholders take action: Influencing, measuring and disclosing how stakeholders are taking action to address nature issues in the value chain requires access to relevant and feasible metrics and data, together with clear guidance on how to apply them consistently across the full value chain. Even when a company has achieved traceability to the sourcing location for certain materials, it still must navigate a complex and rapidly evolving landscape of corporate accountability frameworks – wading through hundreds, if not thousands, of nature-related metrics. In addition to stifling corporate progress, the absence of widely adopted nature-related metrics also limits the ability of financial systems to consistently compare investment opportunities, mitigate potential financial losses due to deterioration of ecosystem services, and contribute to bridging the annual USD 700 billion biodiversity financing gap. 3 A 2025 survey by Responsible Investor cites “data/disclosure gaps” as the second-most significant barrier to nature-positive investment, closely behind “lack of opportunities/products”. 4 The growing landscape of nature-related initiatives and regulations is driving progress on full supply chain traceability and accountability. Yet, most measurement guidance remains high-level and oriented toward actors with direct control over land and ecosystems – typically those operating upstream in the value chain, such as land managers. This focus often overlooks the distinct challenges companies further downstream in the value chain face, such as retailers, who are far removed from the sourcing location where impacts on nature typically occur (see Figure 2 to understand how we define and contextualize the stages of the bio-based value chains in the scope of this report). Downstream companies in particular face structural barriers to setting placed-based targets due to limited visibility and control over activities in their supply chain. As a result, they often rely on metrics that serve as proxies for direct ecological outcomes, reflecting indirect actions or commitments, such as the adoption of certification or supplier commitments that provide a strong foundation to build upon. 01. Introduction As part of WBCSD’s initiative to harmonize the use of nature-related metrics by businesses, the insights in this document explore the specific challenges downstream companies face when sourcing bio-based products from forestry and agriculture. Based on the experience of a working group comprising 24 companies operating in the forest product and agri-food value chains, it assesses existing practices and highlights some of the challenges related to the use of nature-related metrics and setting place-based targets. The insight piece calls for greater recognition of these specific challenges by regulators and standard- setters, and for more detailed and prescriptive guidance to enhance efficiency and strengthen accountability across the full value chain. This insight piece is released alongside an Excel-based metrics tool to support companies downstream in finding metrics that are relevant for their suppliers, and report on their own company’s actions as customers of bio-based products. Nature is facing unprecedented threats. As awareness and regulation of the crisis grow, businesses are intensifying efforts to implement credible and effective strategies to protect and restore the natural world in line with the Kunming- Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework which aims to halt and reverse nature loss by 2030 and achieve full recovery by 2050. However, corporate actions are falling short in addressing the urgency, in part because many companies lack the experience and guidance to manage nature’s inherent complexities. Much of the complexity stems from the need for place-based nature strategies or transition plans targeting the specific ecosystems where impacts and dependencies occur. Unlike climate change, where greenhouse gas emissions are fungible, success in delivering on the Global Biodiversity Framework depends on where and how stakeholders take action to halt and reverse nature loss. → Where impacts occur: Impacts and dependencies on nature frequently occur in a company’s direct operations; but complex value chains, where visibility, predictability and control diminish, also often obscure them.¹ Recognizing this reality, standard- setters and regulators are placing growing demands on companies to measure and disclose nature issues across their entire value chain – particularly in supply chains, where their influence is greatest.²

5 Insights into Nature-related Metrics and Target-setting along the Value Chain 01. Introduction continued Over time, all businesses will likely need to trace, manage and measure their impacts on nature using prescriptive guidance, robust data and harmonized standards. This will include the ability to set strong, place-based targets that align with ecological thresholds and reflect the specific ecosystems where impacts and dependencies occur. In the interim, downstream companies require a practical approach to metric selection and target-setting that enables credible and meaningful action, even in the absence of direct control over nature-related outcomes. WBCSD’s initiative on metrics and target-setting along the value chain In response to growing pressure for companies to mitigate their risks exposure, and measure and disclose nature-related impacts across complex value chains, in 2025 WBCSD convened a working group focused on companies sourcing bio-based products from forest and agri-food value chains. Bio-based products are central to the transition to a nature-positive economy that “results in increasing levels of nature over time, operating within planetary boundaries.” 5 This is because companies directly depend on and influence natural ecosystems – making them both sources of risk for companies and levers for positive nature impact. We selected the forest and agri-food sectors for their critical role in reversing land degradation and conversion, as well as promoting ecosystem restoration while securing the supply of raw materials for the bioeconomy including food, feed, fiber and energy – 99% of which comes from agriculture and forestry. 6 This work builds on WBCSD’s Roadmaps to Nature Positive initiative, specifically the Agri-Food and Forest Products Roadmaps, bringing together peers from the same sector or economic system to define a shared agenda and accelerate credible nature-positive action. These sector- specific approaches provide a foundation for companies to identify key dependencies, impacts, risks, opportunities and priority actions that are essential to contributing to nature-positive outcomes. In a second phase, the same sector groups mapped the actions identified in these Roadmaps to the most relevant and feasible metrics to help companies measure progress, set targets and disclose information in alignment with major voluntary and regulatory frameworks. Where applicable, we linked metrics to targets from existing frameworks, such as the Science Based Targets Network (SBTN). Figure 1 illustrates this two-phased approach – from setting the foundations to identifying metrics and targets. We will make the results of this work accessible via a user-friendly Nature Action Portal , with the first version set to launch in November 2025 (see Annex 2 for details).
