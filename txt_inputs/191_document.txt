Business and Biodiversity This guide is released as a joint publication by IUCN — The World Conservation Union and the World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD). It is a product of a special WBCSD task force and the IUCN Biodiversity Policy Coordination Division. Although the report has been written by the three authors, case study information has been received from members of the WBCSD and non- member companies. In addition, members of the WBCSD and professional staff of IUCN have contributed valuable comments. Although neither the IUCN nor the WBCSD is responsible for the content of the case studies, any queries can be directed to: Giuliana Ammirati at the WBCSD (E-mail: ammirati@wbcsd.ch). The clearance process for the guide has included a review of the text by all members of the WBCSD task force, selected IUCN professional staff, and the final approval of the WBCSD Executive Committee and the IUCN Director General. Although this process ensures that the document incorporates comments by members of both organizations, the views expressed in this publication do not necessarily reflect those of the WBCSD or IUCN. Executive summary The world’s biological resources are used every day by industry – the agricultural, pharmaceutical and forestry industries to mention but three. However, the Earth’s resources are limited and concern has been grow- ing about the way in which they have been managed. Response to a common concern The Convention on Biological Diversity is a major international response to managing the Earth’s biological resources. Its objectives are: • the conservation of biological diversity • the sustainable use of its components • the equitable sharing of its benefits These objectives are extremely broad in scope and have the potential to affect all business sectors by: • restricting long-term access to land and biological resources • imposing more stringent environmental impact assessments • introducing restrictions on the trade in biological products • being of growing concern to investors and other stakeholders The conservation and sustainable use of biological resources remains a major challenge to governments, conservation organizations, the private sector and members of the public. The future needs and objectives of all concerned parties come together on this common ground. Despite the Convention’s potential impact on many business activities, business has not so far been closely involved in the Convention process. Conservation groups, on the other hand, have contributed actively. Unless business takes prompt steps to involve itself, it runs the risk of being excluded from the policy debates now under way – with severe and long-lasting implications for many sectors. 1 A Guide for the Private Sector
