4 Business & Human Rights Ambitions and Actions in India 1 Foreword Home to 1.3 billion people, India is the second largest country by population and the largest democracy in the world. With growth rates continuously above 7% since 2014, India has surpassed China as the fastest growing major economy and is expected to keep this momentum over the next decade. Over the last two years, India moved up 53 points to rank 77 th out of 190 countries in the World Bank’s Ease of Doing Business Index 2019. All of this underscores India’s attractiveness as a key market for local and international companies to do business and invest in. At the same time, India is also home to severe societal and human development challenges. While the Government of India has established various policies, guidelines and corrective actions showing positive results, for example on poverty reduction, India remains one of the front lines of sustainable development. India’s success in achieving the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) on a national level will be crucial to the realization of the SDGs on a global scale. Human rights are an integral element of the SDGs, as there can be no sustainable development in which people are deprived of their fundamental rights and freedoms. And business has a key role to play, consisting of its responsibility to respect human rights – as outlined in the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs) – and of the opportunity to transform people’s lives for the better and make a key contribution towards achieving the SDGs. As economic growth and increased national and international business activity cannot be made at the expense of social and human development, corporate respect for human rights becomes of pivotal importance, especially in the Indian context. It is in this light, that one should read the Government of India’s continued “endeavor to encourage socially responsible businesses”, 1 prominently reflected in its Zero Draft of a National Action Plan on Business and Human Rights and the National Guideline on Responsible Business Conduct. This brief aims to provide WBCSD member companies with operations and business relationships in India with an understanding of the local business and human rights landscape and of stakeholders’ expectations and requirements towards them. 1 Injeti Srinivas, Secretary to Government of India in foreword to the Zero Draft of the Indian National Action Plan on Business and Human Rights http://mca.gov.in/Ministry/pdf/NationalPlanBusinessHumanRight_13022019.pdf 4 Business & Human Rights Ambitions and Actions in India
