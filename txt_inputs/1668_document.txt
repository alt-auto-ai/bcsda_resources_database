Why integrated transition planning matters As climate, nature, and social factors increasingly interact, companies face new perspectives in how they protect, create, and measure value. With markets, policy, and finance evolving rapidly, expectations are rising for credible, integrated plans that connect these priorities - not only to advance sustainability, but to build resilience and competitiveness in a changing world. Integrated transition planning enables companies to: • Improve governance and build stakeholder confidence by strengthening how material impacts, risks and opportunities are assessed. • Unlock efficiencies and new opportunities through coordinated actions, reduced duplication and smarter resource allocation. • Strengthen resilience and reliability , maintaining operations and customer trust even during disruptions. • Enhance overall effectiveness as integrated actions deliver stronger outcomes than siloed efforts. • Capture near-term value — cost savings, risk reduction and revenue opportunities — while building long-term strategic advantage. • Align strategy and capital allocation by linking climate, nature and social priorities to investment decisions and enterprise-wide planning. Introduction Inside this primer • What integration delivers - clearer efficiencies, new opportunities, stronger investor confidence, improved resilience, and better management of trade-offs. • The evolving context - rising physical risks, deepening nature dependencies, increasing expectations from investors, regulators and stakeholders. • How integration works in practice - shifting from siloed climate-only work to a unified strategy across mitigation, adaptation, nature, and social/just transition. • Foundations for action - practical early steps, decision rules, KPIs, and alignment across functions and value chains. • Real-world examples - case studies from finance, consumer goods, utilities, and industry demonstrating integrated climate-nature-social action. • What’s next & how to prepare - the road ahead for integrated transition planning plus a readiness checklist helping companies assess ambition, governance, data systems, capabilities, and resourcing before starting. Integrated transition planning is emerging as a critical driver of business resilience and value, providing a pragmatic way to combine climate, nature and social considerations into a decision-useful approach for strategic and operational decision-making. This introductory guide explains why integration matters, how it works, what it delivers, and how to get started. 01. A changing risk landscape How accelerating risks and shifting dependencies are reshaping the business environment 03. Physical risks are intensifying Seven planetary boundaries have been breached, climate and environmental change is no longer a distant threat, acute and chronic risks and direct and network effects are already disrupting value chains, markets, and communities. In 2024 alone, climate-related disasters caused an estimated $327 bn in losses, with more than half uninsured 1 . Even under a net-zero trajectory, chronic physical risks remain material: rising temperatures, shifting rainfall patterns and sea-level rise could reduce global GDP by up to 8.5% 2 . Transition planning has traditionally focused on mitigation, but escalating physical risks mean businesses must now embed adaptation and resilience into core strategies to protect operations and assets from future shocks. Nature is a material business dependency Businesses rely on ecosystem services that provide essential inputs — raw materials, water, pollination, stable soils and climate regulation. Economic activity is degrading these same ecosystems, reducing nature’s ability to deliver the services that underpin business continuity. This is not only an environmental issue; it is a material financial risk as over 55% of global GDP ($58 trillion) is now estimated to be moderately or highly dependent on nature 3 . As recognition of nature’s critical economic role grows, companies must integrate nature into business strategies, understanding that these systems are interdependent and must be managed together to safeguard long-term resilience. Expectations are rising Standards, stakeholders and capital markets are increasingly demanding credible, people-centered and integrated transition plans 03. Transition finance opportunities Despite mounting pressures, the momentum for transition planning remains robust. The market is rewarding execution and credible delivery – not just ambition. • 37% of investors already allocate funds to companies with high emissions but credible transition plans, with another 26% planning to do so in the next 1-2 years. 11 • 77% of financial institutions find portfolio companies more attractive when they maintain transition plans. 11 • Nature finance is emerging as a major investment opportunity, with global institutions mobilizing new capital as the nature-positive transition is estimated to require an additional US$484 billion per year in investment. 12 • Just transition finance is accelerating, with investors recognizing its value as strategies that embed social inclusion can reduce project-level risk premiums by 10 – 20% and strengthen long-term investment performance. 13 Importance of people-centered approach Managed without a people-centered lens, climate and nature transitions can trigger job losses, inequality and community disruption 7,8 . Effective plans anticipate cultural, political and behavioral responses – so support is built rather than eroded. The economic stakes are significant, climate-driven health risks alone could strip at least $1.5 tn from global productivity by 2050 9 . The WEF Global Risks Report 2025 10 highlights inequality and societal polarization as central challenges, and recent convenings including the Second World Summit for Social Development, renewed calls for people-centered, rights-based approaches.
