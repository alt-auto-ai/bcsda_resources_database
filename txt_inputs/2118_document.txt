2 Swiss Re Institute sigma No 1/2025 Last year’s insured losses from natural catastrophes were on trend at USD 137 billion. Following the long-term trend of 5 ‒ 7% annual increase in real terms, insured losses from natural catastrophes will approach USD 145 billion in 2025. This year started with wildfires sweeping across the Los Angeles area in January. The currently estimated USD 40 billion of resultant insured losses is a record high, but the fires on their own will not be cause of notable deviation from the annual loss growth trend. The scale of losses elicited a reinsurance response, with two thirds of the claims payouts coming from primary insurers, and the rest from reinsurers. As has been the case in recent years, in 2024 most of the global insured losses were driven by secondary perils, in particular severe convective storms in the US. Most of the losses from the higher-frequency secondary perils have been covered by insurers. However, primary perils carry most loss potential, and when they strike urban areas with large asset values, the loss outcome of all natural disasters in that year can be well above trend. Think, for example, of the scale of devastation wreaked by Hurricane Katrina in New Orleans in 2005. These are what we call “peak loss” years, and there have been five peak loss years in the last 30. It is in peak loss years that utility of reinsurance comes to the fore, stepping in to absorb a large share of the peak event losses. The last peak loss year was 2017, when hurricanes Harvey, Irma and Maria drove global insurance losses to 111% above trend. The period of quiet since then did not slow the underlying growth of risk. Using a model-based approach, we estimate a 1-in-10 probability that 2025 total insured losses reach USD 300 billion or more. Assuming the 5 ‒ 7% annual growth trend for insured losses continues, the 1-in-10 peak year loss scenario will increase beyond that level in the coming years. With advanced markets’ primary insurance capital estimated at around USD 1.8 trillion for 2024 and traditional reinsurance capital at USD 500 billion, the re/insurance market is currently well-equipped to cover such a loss. Reinsurance plays a critical role as a shock absorber of peak loss year volatility and is expected to cover more than half of global losses above annual trend. To maintain this resilience, it is essential that growth in both traditional and alternative reinsurance capital keeps pace with rising natural catastrophe risks. The LA fires bring another theme to the fore, increases in insurance claims costs. In the US, net incurred losses of homeowner insurers have increased by 8% annually since 2018, driven by a post-COVID surge in construction and claims costs. The consequence of the latter is that homeowner insurers have seen several years of underwriting losses. In the long run and across geographies, a main factor determining premium rates is exposure to natural perils. We find that in California, the highest premiums are localised in areas with greatest exposures to wildfire risk. And countrywide, we see a strong correlation between average catastrophe losses per policy and homeowner premiums at state level. Increasing exposures in areas of high natural perils risk challenge insurability, highlighting the need for loss mitigation and adaption. The insurance industry is an intrinsic part of the solution to close protection gaps as natural catastrophes losses continue to trend higher. To do so sustainably, the call is to reduce loss potential from the outset with effective prevention and adaptation measures, which will also help reduce the cost of re/insurance and maintain the viability of risk transfer business. There have been cases where adaptation measures have facilitated lower premiums. Important is that adaptation and mitigation measures require a multi- stakeholder approach, including individual property owners, governments, regulators, supervisors and the insurance industry. Executive summary Insured losses from natural catastrophe are set to trend to USD 145 billion in 2025. Primary perils carry most loss potential. In peak loss years, they can drive insured losses to more than double trend. We estimate a 1-in-10 probability that 2025 total insured losses reach USD 300 billion or more. The recent increase in net incurred losses of homeowner insurers in the US reflects economic realities and the impact of increasing catastrophe risk. Ultimately, however, as losses continue to rise, more prevention and adaption needs to be actioned.

sigma No 1/2025 Swiss Re Institute 5 Insured losses trending towards USD 145 billion in 2025 Global insured losses from natural catastrophes reached USD 137 billion in 2024, the 5 th year running in which insured losses were above USD 100 billion (see Figure 1). Among a multitude of perils, the disasters contributing most to the accumulation of losses were hurricanes Helene and Milton, severe convective storms (SCS) in the US, large-scale urban floods around the world and the highest ever recorded natural catastrophe insured losses in Canada. Global economic losses from catastrophes were USD 318 billion, the highest since 2017 (USD 448 billion in 2024 prices). Around 43% were covered by insurance, highlighting the continued existence of large protection gaps in many parts of the world, including in advanced economies. Though higher than in 2023, last year’s insured losses could have been worse. In the absence of a major primary peril event (namely an earthquake or a big tropical cyclone loss), the 2024 loss total was not exceptional. Rather it matched the USD 137 billion as estimated by the long-term trend of a 5 ‒ 7% annual increase in insured losses in real terms (see Figure 2). Insured losses on trend Global natural catastrophe insured losses were on trend in 2024, coming in at USD 137 billion. The main drivers were Hurricanes Helene and Milton, and severe convective storms. Secondary perils made the biggest contribution to global losses, and the dramatic fires in Los Angeles in January this year point to another year of high losses from this category in 2025. If the average annual 5 ‒ 7% increase in losses holds, insured losses will trend towards USD 145 billion in 2025. In recent years, the pattern has been that secondary perils have contributed more to annual insured losses than have primary perils. However, primary perils still hold the largest loss potential. History shows that just one major primary peril event like a tropical cyclone striking an urban area with high population and property concentration can trigger losses many multiples higher than trend.
