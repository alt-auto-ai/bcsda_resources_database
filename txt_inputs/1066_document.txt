Overview Pharmaceutical sector: Priority actions towards a nature-positive future October 2024

Introduction Nature is critical to human health and well-being, providing us with air, water, food and raw materials, including ingredients used in medicines and medicinal compounds. Nature loss, climate change and pollution are therefore directly endangering the health of people as well as the planet. The WHO estimates that nearly 14 million people die each year from environmental health risks 1 . The pharmaceutical sector, which exists to improve human health, is resource intensive. While the continuing advancement of medical technology has, in part, led to the extension of global life expectancy from 46.5 years in 1950 to 71.2 years in 2023 2 , the development, manufacture and delivery of medicines are, con - versely, also contributing to climate change and nature loss. For example, it is estimated that healthcare systems are responsible for around 5% of global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions 3 . Climate change and nature loss have a range of adverse consequences for human health, including: • The emergence of new zoonotic diseases, as habitat loss brings society in closer proximity to nature. Already, land use change has resulted in the emergence of more than 30% of new diseases reported since 1960 4 . • Exposure to extreme weather events leading to shortages of food, water, emergence of vector-borne diseases, and the exacerbation of chronic conditions like drought. • Climate change and biodiversity loss resulting in changing patterns of diseases. • Worsening human health from the effects of heat and air pollution. • Negative impacts on mental health and mental distress associ - ated with worsening environmental conditions and nature loss 5 . The complexity of pharmaceutical value chains presents unique challenges in identifying and managing deeply embedded nature-related impacts and dependencies. Examples of these challenges include the procurement of inorganic and organic feedstock and raw materials upstream, or the end-of-life treatment of pharmaceutical products downstream, where traceability is difficult to maintain, and operational influence is limited. Additionally, in order to maintain the highest medical effi - cacy and safety standards, medicines are manufactured in energy and water-intensive facilities and single-use items are prevalent. Making changes to products and processes and developing innovative solutions to minimize these impacts requires time, as well as collaboration with regulators to ensure patient safety is maintained. To complement ongoing sustainability initiatives, businesses across the sector should use The Nature Strategy Handbook – an accessible tool drawing on the A ssess, C ommit, T ransform, and D isclose framework ( ACT-D high-level business actions on nature ) to help businesses understand their relationship with nature and develop a credible nature strategy. The Handbook helps companies to acknowledge the value of nature to their business; assess and measure their impacts and dependencies on nature; set transparent, time-bound, science-based targets; take actions to address their key impacts and dependencies; and publicly disclose performance and other relevant nature-related information. This overview provides a non-exhaustive, sector-level sum - mary of potential key impacts and dependencies on nature. Importantly, it also sets out the priority actions that leading businesses in the sector should take now to transform and ensure the pharmaceutical sector plays its part in meeting the global goal to halt and reverse nature loss by 2030 - the mission at the heart of the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (also referred to as the Biodiversity Plan.) Scope of this overview This document covers the full value chain of the human pharmaceutical sector (SICS CODE: HC-BP), from raw material production and manufacturing to processing and distribution of pharmaceutical products, customer usage and end-of-life disposal. While this overview does not include specific considerations for veterinary medicine, some of the contents in this document may be of use to businesses in that industry. 2

Pharmaceutical value chain The pharmaceutical value chain is long and complex, spanning numerous industries as outlined below: • Upstream: Sourcing raw materials from organic (agricultural and natural systems) and inorganic (mined metals and minerals) compounds. • Operations: The construction and use of manufacturing sites and offices, including the sourcing of paper and packaging. • Downstream: The distribution, use and disposal of pharmaceu - tical products. Nature-related impacts i To protect and enhance ecosystems on which they depend, businesses in the pharmaceutical sector are encouraged to direct their efforts towards addressing the most significant impacts on nature in their operations and value chains, namely (in no specific order): • Water use – Water is used as an ingredient in medicines, in production processes (e.g., as a solvent for cooling), and for the cleaning and sterilization of labs and equipment 6 . Both groundwater and surface water are used. The production of both specialist and commodity agricultural crops used by the sector can also be water intensive, as can extractive industries in the pharmaceutical value chain. • Water pollution – The release of Active Pharmaceutical Ingredients (APIs; including antibiotics), harmful chemicals and other substances ii can occur due to activities across the pharma - ceutical value chain, including from patient use and improper disposal of unused medicines. Tight regulation and controls are applied to prevent the release of APIs in waterways, however some discharges into water can occur in the manufacturing process. For example, antibiotic releases into the environment can contribute to antimicrobial resistance (one of the WHO’s top 10 threats to global public health) 7 . • GHG and non-GHG emissions – Manufacturing processes which require specific ambient conditions - such as humidity, temper - ature and sterility - along with company vehicles and purchased energy, all contribute to direct operational GHG emissions. Additionally, sourcing inputs upstream, as well as product distribution, transport and usage downstream, all significantly contribute to value chain emissions 8 . Non-GHG emissions are also linked to the pharmaceutical industry iii . These emissions come from the materials used and the chemical processes involved, often resulting in air pollution, wastewater and solid waste from leftover materials 9 . • Land use change – Pharmaceutical manufacturing can lead to some changes in land use. These occur midstream with the construction of new manufacturing facilities or offices, but even more significantly upstream due to the procurement of chemical intermediary products. These intermediary products are often derived either from agricultural or forestry-based feedstocks such as palm oil, soy or sugarcane, or as inorganic materials extracted from mines or quarries, all of which significantly impact land use. • Solid waste – The waste streams generated from manufac - turing pharmaceuticals contain a mix of hazardous materials including solvents and chemicals, and plastics such as product packaging and single-use plastics, some of which have come in contact with biological or genetically modified materials. Solid waste is generated from delivery mechanisms such as single-use syringes, insulin cartridges and asthma inhalers. Nature-related dependencies iv Like many sectors, the pharmaceutical sector is dependent on a number of ecosystem services and assets to operate. To understand their relationship with nature, businesses should identify these dependencies to support the evaluation of nature-related risks and opportunities. These dependencies strengthen the business case to invest in the protection and restoration of nature. Businesses in the sector mainly rely on (in no specific order): • Water supply – Pharmaceutical manufacturing processes and the use of water as a product ingredient, such as water for injection (WFI), rely on a steady, pure and abundant supply of freshwater 11 . Climate change-related impacts on precipitation patterns and surface water evaporation, along with increased water consumption due to human activities, can contribute to depleted water supplies. • Water purification – Despite heavily regulated systems reducing water pollutants in direct operations, the pharmaceutical sector’s value chain still relies on bioremediation and filtration to miti - gate environmental impacts from the release of APIs, harmful chemicals and other substances. Natural physical and biological processes help remove pollutants and waste from agricultural, industrial and domestic sources 12 . • Climate regulation – The pharmaceutical sector has a long, highly varied and complex value chain that depends on a stable climate. Climate hazards have the potential to disrupt crop growth, the mining of key metals, and the transportation of these products by road and sea. Climate regulation is necessary to prevent disruptions to manufacturing operations and down - stream product delivery. Climatic variability and extreme climate events can also impact health due to increased temperature, food insecurity and malnutrition, and the transmission and spread of certain diseases. • Biomass (natural products from both renewable and non-renewable sources) – The pharmaceutical sector relies on biomass inputs for production processes, and increasingly relies on biomass for energy generation. For example, Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL) derived from the blood of horseshoe crabs is used in a test for endotoxins in pharmaceutical products which is critical for testing patient safety 13 . Its use is mandated by regulatory controls in some jurisdictions. Similarly, many phar - maceutical products require natural ingredients derived from agricultural products such as corn, soy or other wild species, while packaging is reliant on wood fiber for paper production. • Research and use of genetic material – Genetic information from non-human organisms and viruses can be used in research and development to improve the scientific understanding of diseases. This can lead to insights and innovations that contribute to the development of new medications, vaccines and improved biotechnology processes. Target 13 of the Global Biodiversity Framework covers the fair use, procurement and manage - ment of genetic and biological resources, to ensure equitable benefit sharing. 5
